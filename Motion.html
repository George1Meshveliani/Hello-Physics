Position, Distance, and Displacement

Before we measure speed, we need to know where things are and where they're going.

Key Terms

Position (\(x\)): An object's specific location.

Distance: The total length of the path you travel. (Like the reading on a car's odometer).

Displacement (\(\Delta x\)): The straight-line change in position from start to end. Direction matters!

Speed vs. Velocity

They seem similar, but in physics, direction is the key difference!

Speed (Scalar)

How fast you are moving, regardless of direction.

\[ \text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} \]

Velocity (Vector)

The rate your position is changing. It's speed in a specific direction.

\[ \text{Velocity} = \frac{\text{Displacement}}{\text{Time}} \]

Race Track Example

A car completes one lap on a 1-mile track. Its average speed might be 120 mph, but its average velocity is 0 mph because its displacement is zero!

Acceleration: The Rate of Change

Acceleration is any change in velocity. This can be speeding up, slowing down, or just changing direction!

\[ \text{Acceleration} = \frac{\text{Change in Velocity}}{\text{Time}} = \frac{v_{final} - v_{initial}}{t} \]

Speeding Up

Positive acceleration.

Slowing Down

Negative acceleration.

Changing Direction

Even at constant speed!

The BIG Four: Formulas of Motion

For objects moving with constant acceleration, these four formulas are your best friends.

Variables:

  • \(\Delta x\): displacement
  • \(v_0\): initial velocity
  • \(v\): final velocity
  • \(a\): acceleration
  • \(t\): time
  1. \(v = v_0 + at\)
  2. \(\Delta x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
  3. \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x\)
  4. \(\Delta x = \frac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)t\)

Using the Formulas: An Example

A car, starting from rest, accelerates at a constant 2 m/sÂ² for 5 seconds.

What is its final velocity?

We need \(v\). We know \(v_0=0\), \(a=2\), \(t=5\). The formula without \(\Delta x\) is perfect.

\[v = v_0 + at \rightarrow v = 0 + (2)(5) = 10 \text{ m/s}\]

How far did it travel?

We need \(\Delta x\). We know \(v_0=0\), \(a=2\), \(t=5\). The formula without \(v\) works well.

\[\Delta x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \rightarrow \Delta x = (0)(5) + \frac{1}{2}(2)(5)^2 = 25 \text{ m}\]

Problem Set: Group 1

Problem 1

A sprinter runs 100 meters in 10 seconds. What is her average speed?

Problem 2

A bike starting from rest accelerates at 1 m/sÂ² for 4 seconds. What is its final velocity?

Solutions: Group 1

Problem 1: Sprinter's Speed

Formula: \( \text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} \)

Calculation: \( \text{Speed} = \frac{100 \text{ m}}{10 \text{ s}} \)

Answer: 10 m/s

Problem 2: Bike's Velocity

Given: \(v_0 = 0\) (from rest), \(a = 1\) m/sÂ², \(t = 4\) s

Formula: \(v = v_0 + at\)

Calculation: \(v = 0 + (1 \text{ m/sÂ²})(4 \text{ s})\)

Answer: 4 m/s

Problem Set: Group 2

Problem 1

A train traveling at 30 m/s applies its brakes and slows to 10 m/s in 5 seconds. What is its acceleration?

Problem 2

A ball is dropped from a tall building. How far does it fall in 3 seconds? (Use g = 9.8 m/sÂ² and ignore air resistance).

Solutions: Group 2

Problem 1: Train's Acceleration

Given: \(v_0 = 30\) m/s, \(v = 10\) m/s, \(t = 5\) s

Formula: \(a = (v - v_0) / t\)

Calculation: \(a = (10 \text{ m/s} - 30 \text{ m/s}) / 5 \text{ s}\)

Answer: -4 m/sÂ² (The negative sign indicates deceleration)

Problem 2: Falling Ball

Given: \(v_0 = 0\) (dropped), \(a = g = 9.8\) m/sÂ², \(t = 3\) s

Formula: \(\Delta x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

Calculation: \(\Delta x = (0)(3) + \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(3)^2 = 0 + 4.9 \times 9\)

Answer: 44.1 meters

Problem Set: Group 3

Problem 1

A car accelerates from rest at a constant rate of 4 m/sÂ². How much distance does it cover before it reaches a speed of 20 m/s?

Problem 2

A stone is thrown straight upwards with an initial velocity of 19.6 m/s. What is the maximum height it reaches? (Use g = 9.8 m/sÂ²).

Solutions: Group 3

Problem 1: Car's Distance

Given: \(v_0 = 0\), \(a = 4\) m/sÂ², \(v = 20\) m/s

Formula: \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x\)

Calculation: \((20)^2 = (0)^2 + 2(4)\Delta x \rightarrow 400 = 8\Delta x\)

Answer: \(\Delta x = 50\) meters

Problem 2: Stone's Maximum Height

Given: \(v_0 = 19.6\) m/s, \(v = 0\) (at max height), \(a = -g = -9.8\) m/sÂ²

Formula: \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x\)

Calculation: \((0)^2 = (19.6)^2 + 2(-9.8)\Delta x \rightarrow 0 = 384.16 - 19.6\Delta x\)

Answer: \(\Delta x = 19.6\) meters
