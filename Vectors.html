Vectors: The Language of Physics

A comprehensive guide to vector representation, algebra, products, and calculus.

1. Scalars and Vectors: The Difference

Scalar Quantity

A quantity defined only by its magnitude (a number and a unit). Scalars follow the rules of ordinary arithmetic.

Examples: Mass ($\text{kg}$), Temperature ($\text{K}$), Time ($\text{s}$).

Vector Quantity

A quantity defined by both its magnitude and its direction in space. Vectors follow specialized rules of algebra.

Examples: Force ($\vec{F}$), Velocity ($\vec{v}$), Displacement ($\Delta \vec{r}$).

2. Representation and Vector Algebra

2.1. Component Representation

A vector $\vec{A}$ in a Cartesian coordinate system can be expressed as a linear combination of the unit vectors ($\hat{i}$, $\hat{j}$, $\hat{k}$).

$$\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j} + A_z \hat{k} \quad \text{(2.1)}$$

The magnitude (length) of $\vec{A}$ is found using the Pythagorean theorem:

$$|\vec{A}| = A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2 + A_z^2} \quad \text{(2.2)}$$

2.2. Vector Addition and Subtraction

Vectors are added or subtracted by combining their respective components.

Vector Sum ($\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$):

$$\vec{R} = (A_x + B_x) \hat{i} + (A_y + B_y) \hat{j} + (A_z + B_z) \hat{k} \quad \text{(2.3)}$$

Illustration of Vector Addition (Head-to-Tail Rule).

3. Vector Products: Dot and Cross

3.1. The Scalar Product (Dot Product)

The dot product of $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ yields a scalar quantity. It measures the extent to which $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ are parallel.

Geometric Definition:

$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = |\vec{A}| |\vec{B}| \cos \theta \quad \text{(3.1)}$$

Component Definition:

$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_y + A_z B_z \quad \text{(3.2)}$$

Physics Example: Work ($W$)

Work is the scalar product of force ($\vec{F}$) and displacement ($\vec{d}$):

$$W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d} = |\vec{F}| |\vec{d}| \cos \theta$$

3.2. The Vector Product (Cross Product)

The cross product of $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ yields a vector quantity, $\vec{C}$, perpendicular to the plane containing $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$. The direction is determined by the Right-Hand Rule.

Illustration of the Cross Product $\vec{A} \times \vec{B}$ and the Right-Hand Rule.

Geometric Definition (Magnitude):

$$|\vec{A} \times \vec{B}| = |\vec{A}| |\vec{B}| \sin \theta \quad \text{(3.3)}$$

Component Definition (Determinant Form):

$$\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ A_x & A_y & A_z \\ B_x & B_y & B_z \end{vmatrix} \quad \text{(3.4)}$$

Physics Example: Torque ($\vec{\tau}$)

Torque is the vector product of the position vector ($\vec{r}$) and the force ($\vec{F}$):

$$\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$$

4. Vector Calculus Fundamentals: Del Operator

The Del operator ($\nabla$, sometimes called *nabla*) is a fundamental vector differential operator used to describe how scalar and vector fields change in space. In Cartesian coordinates, it is defined as:

4.1. The Del Operator ($\nabla$)

$$\nabla = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\hat{i} + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\hat{j} + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\hat{k} \quad \text{(4.1)}$$

4.2. Gradient ($\nabla f$): Acting on a Scalar Field

The gradient of a scalar field $f(x, y, z)$ (like temperature or electric potential) is a vector that points in the direction of the greatest rate of increase of the field, and its magnitude is that maximum rate.

Definition:

$$\text{grad } f = \nabla f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\hat{i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\hat{j} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\hat{k} \quad \text{(4.2)}$$

The Gradient ($\nabla T$) vector points perpendicular to the level curves (contours), indicating the steepest rate of change.

Physics Example: Force and Potential Energy

For a conservative force $\vec{F}$, the force vector is the negative gradient of the potential energy function $U$:

$$\vec{F} = - \nabla U$$

4.3. Divergence ($\nabla \cdot \vec{F}$): Acting on a Vector Field

The divergence of a vector field $\vec{F}$ (like fluid velocity or electric field) is a scalar quantity. It measures the rate at which flux (flow) is expanding or contracting at a given point (source or sink).

Definition:

$$\text{div } \vec{F} = \nabla \cdot \vec{F} = \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial z} \quad \text{(4.3)}$$

Physics Example: Gauss's Law for Electric Fields

In differential form, Gauss's law relates the electric field divergence to the charge density $\rho$:

$$\nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \quad (\text{Maxwell's First Equation})$$

A positive divergence means the point is a source (positive charge).

4.4. Curl ($\nabla \times \vec{F}$): Acting on a Vector Field

The curl of a vector field $\vec{F}$ is a vector quantity. It measures the circulation or rotation (vorticity) of the field at a given point. The curl vector points along the axis of rotation.

Definition (Determinant Form):

$$\text{curl } \vec{F} = \nabla \times \vec{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ F_x & F_y & F_z \end{vmatrix} \quad \text{(4.4)}$$

Expanded Component Form:

$$\nabla \times \vec{F} = \left(\frac{\partial F_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial z}\right)\hat{i} + \left(\frac{\partial F_x}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial x}\right)\hat{j} + \left(\frac{\partial F_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial y}\right)\hat{k}$$

The Curl ($\nabla \times \vec{F}$) vector indicates the axis and magnitude of rotation (vorticity) in a vector field.

Physics Example: Ampère's Law (Magnetic Field)

The curl of the magnetic field ($\vec{B}$) is related to the current density ($\vec{J}$):

$$\nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} \quad (\text{Ignoring displacement current})$$

5. Key Vector Proofs and Properties

5.1. Proof: Dot Product Commutativity

The dot product is commutative, meaning the order of the vectors does not matter: $\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A}$.

Proof:

From the geometric definition: $\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = |\vec{A}| |\vec{B}| \cos \theta$.

Since $\cos \theta = \cos (-\theta)$, the angle measured from $\vec{A}$ to $\vec{B}$ is equivalent in the cosine function to the angle measured from $\vec{B}$ to $\vec{A}$ (the vectors' magnitudes are scalars and also commute).

Using the component form:

$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_y + A_z B_z$$ $$\vec{B} \cdot \vec{A} = B_x A_x + B_y A_y + B_z A_z$$

Since the multiplication of scalar components is commutative ($A_x B_x = B_x A_x$), we have:

$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A}$$

5.2. Proof: Cross Product Anti-Commutativity

The cross product is anti-commutative, meaning reversing the order reverses the direction: $\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = -(\vec{B} \times \vec{A})$.

Proof:

From the geometric definition, the magnitude remains the same: $|\vec{A} \times \vec{B}| = |\vec{A}| |\vec{B}| \sin \theta$.

However, the direction is determined by the Right-Hand Rule:

  • Fingers curl from $\vec{A}$ to $\vec{B}$: Thumb points in the direction of $\vec{A} \times \vec{B}$.
  • Fingers curl from $\vec{B}$ to $\vec{A}$: Thumb points in the exact opposite direction.

Using the component form (3.4):

$$\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = (A_y B_z - A_z B_y)\hat{i} + (A_z B_x - A_x B_z)\hat{j} + (A_x B_y - A_y B_x)\hat{k}$$ $$\vec{B} \times \vec{A} = (B_y A_z - B_z A_y)\hat{i} + (B_z A_x - B_x A_z)\hat{j} + (B_x A_y - B_y A_x)\hat{k}$$

Factoring out $-1$ from $\vec{B} \times \vec{A}$ shows the relation:

$$\vec{B} \times \vec{A} = -[(A_z B_y - A_y B_z)\hat{i} + (A_x B_z - A_z B_x)\hat{j} + (A_y B_x - A_x B_y)\hat{k}]$$ $$\vec{B} \times \vec{A} = -(\vec{A} \times \vec{B})$$

6. Practice Problems and Solutions

6.1. Problems

Intermediate (5 Problems)

  1. Vector Addition & Unit Vector: Given $\vec{A} = 3\hat{i} - 2\hat{j} + 4\hat{k}$ and $\vec{B} = -\hat{i} + 5\hat{j} - \hat{k}$, find the magnitude of the resultant vector $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$ and the unit vector in the direction of $\vec{R}$.
  2. Dot Product and Angle: Find the angle $\theta$ between the vectors $\vec{A} = 2\hat{i} + 2\hat{j} - \hat{k}$ and $\vec{B} = 6\hat{i} - 3\hat{j} + 2\hat{k}$.
  3. Cross Product and Area: Calculate the magnitude of the cross product of $\vec{A} = 3\hat{i} + 0\hat{j} + 4\hat{k}$ and $\vec{B} = 0\hat{i} + 5\hat{j} + 0\hat{k}$. What physical quantity does this magnitude represent?
  4. Projection and Work: A force $\vec{F} = (5\hat{i} - 2\hat{j}) \text{ N}$ is applied to move an object along the displacement vector $\vec{d} = (3\hat{i} + 4\hat{j}) \text{ m}$. Calculate the work done, $W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d}$.
  5. Physics Application (Torque): A force $\vec{F} = (2\hat{i} + \hat{j}) \text{ N}$ is applied at a position $\vec{r} = (3\hat{j} - \hat{k}) \text{ m}$. Calculate the resulting torque vector $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$.

Advanced (3 Problems)

  1. Gradient and Directional Derivative: Given the scalar temperature field $T(x, y, z) = x^2 y - 3yz$, calculate the Gradient ($\nabla T$) at the point $P(1, 1, 1)$. Then, find the directional derivative of $T$ at $P$ in the direction of the vector $\vec{v} = \hat{i} + \hat{j} + \hat{k}$.
  2. Divergence (Source/Sink): Calculate the Divergence ($\nabla \cdot \vec{F}$) of the velocity field $\vec{v} = (x^3 + 2xy)\hat{i} + (y^3 - 2yz)\hat{j} + (z^3 + 3zx)\hat{k}$. Is the field solenoidal (divergence-free)?
  3. Curl (Rotation): A vector field is given by $\vec{F} = (y^2)\hat{i} + (z^2)\hat{j} + (x^2)\hat{k}$. Calculate the Curl ($\nabla \times \vec{F}$) of this field.

Irodov-like (2 Problems)

  1. Test for Conservative Field: A force field is given by $\vec{F} = (2xyz + y^2)\hat{i} + (x^2z + 2xy)\hat{j} + (x^2y)\hat{k}$. Determine if this force field is conservative by calculating the Curl. (A conservative field must have $\nabla \times \vec{F} = 0$).
  2. Vector Identity Proof: Prove the vector identity: Divergence of the Curl is Zero, i.e., $\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{F}) = 0$, for any arbitrary vector field $\vec{F} = F_x \hat{i} + F_y \hat{j} + F_z \hat{k}$. Assume the second partial derivatives are continuous.

6.2. Solutions

Problem 1: Vector Addition & Unit Vector

1. Find the resultant vector $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$:

$$\vec{R} = (3 - 1)\hat{i} + (-2 + 5)\hat{j} + (4 - 1)\hat{k} = 2\hat{i} + 3\hat{j} + 3\hat{k}$$

2. Calculate the magnitude $|\vec{R}|$:

$$|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{2^2 + 3^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{4 + 9 + 9} = \sqrt{22}$$

3. Find the unit vector $\hat{r}$:

$$\hat{r} = \frac{\vec{R}}{|\vec{R}|} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{22}}(2\hat{i} + 3\hat{j} + 3\hat{k})$$

Problem 2: Angle Calculation

1. Calculate $\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = (2)(6) + (2)(-3) + (-1)(2) = 12 - 6 - 2 = 4$

2. Calculate magnitudes: $|\vec{A}| = \sqrt{2^2 + 2^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{9} = 3$, $|\vec{B}| = \sqrt{6^2 + (-3)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{49} = 7$

3. Calculate angle:

$$\cos \theta = \frac{4}{(3)(7)} = \frac{4}{21}$$ $$\theta = \arccos\left(\frac{4}{21}\right) \approx 79.0^\circ$$

Problem 3: Cross Product and Area

1. Calculate $\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 3 & 0 & 4 \\ 0 & 5 & 0 \end{vmatrix} = (0-20)\hat{i} - (0-0)\hat{j} + (15-0)\hat{k} = -20\hat{i} + 15\hat{k}$

2. Calculate magnitude: $|\vec{A} \times \vec{B}| = \sqrt{(-20)^2 + 15^2} = \sqrt{400 + 225} = \sqrt{625} = 25$

Physical Quantity:

The magnitude $|\vec{A} \times \vec{B}|$ represents the area of the parallelogram formed by vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$.

Problem 4: Projection and Work

The work done is the dot product $W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d}$:

$$W = (5\hat{i} - 2\hat{j}) \cdot (3\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})$$ $$W = (5)(3) + (-2)(4) = 15 - 8 = 7 \text{ J}$$

Problem 5: Torque Application

Calculate the cross product $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$:

$$\vec{\tau} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 0 & 3 & -1 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \end{vmatrix} = (0 - (-1))\hat{i} - (0 - (-2))\hat{j} + (0 - 6)\hat{k}$$ $$\vec{\tau} = \hat{i} - 2\hat{j} - 6\hat{k} \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$$

Problem 6: Gradient and Directional Derivative

1. Calculate the partial derivatives of $T = x^2 y - 3yz$:

$$\frac{\partial T}{\partial x} = 2xy, \quad \frac{\partial T}{\partial y} = x^2 - 3z, \quad \frac{\partial T}{\partial z} = -3y$$

2. Form the Gradient and evaluate at $P(1, 1, 1)$:

$$\nabla T |_{(1,1,1)} = 2(1)(1)\hat{i} + (1^2 - 3(1))\hat{j} - 3(1)\hat{k} = 2\hat{i} - 2\hat{j} - 3\hat{k}$$

3. Find the unit vector in the direction of $\vec{v}$: $\hat{v} = \frac{\hat{i} + \hat{j} + \hat{k}}{\sqrt{1^2+1^2+1^2}} = \frac{\hat{i} + \hat{j} + \hat{k}}{\sqrt{3}}$

4. Calculate the directional derivative, $D_{\hat{v}} T = \nabla T \cdot \hat{v}$:

$$D_{\hat{v}} T = (2\hat{i} - 2\hat{j} - 3\hat{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right)(\hat{i} + \hat{j} + \hat{k}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(2(1) - 2(1) - 3(1))$$ $$D_{\hat{v}} T = \frac{-3}{\sqrt{3}} = -\sqrt{3}$$

Problem 7: Divergence (Source/Sink)

Calculate the Divergence $\nabla \cdot \vec{v}$:

$$\nabla \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial z}$$ $$\nabla \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^3 + 2xy) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y^3 - 2yz) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(z^3 + 3zx)$$ $$\nabla \cdot \vec{v} = (3x^2 + 2y) + (3y^2 - 2z) + (3z^2 + 0)$$ $$\nabla \cdot \vec{v} = 3x^2 + 3y^2 + 3z^2 + 2y - 2z$$

Since $\nabla \cdot \vec{v}$ is not generally zero, the field is not solenoidal. It has sources and sinks that vary with position.

Problem 8: Curl (Rotation)

Calculate the Curl $\nabla \times \vec{F}$ using the determinant:

$$\nabla \times \vec{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ y^2 & z^2 & x^2 \end{vmatrix}$$ $$\nabla \times \vec{F} = \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(z^2)\right)\hat{i} - \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(y^2)\right)\hat{j} + \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(z^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y^2)\right)\hat{k}$$ $$\nabla \times \vec{F} = (0 - 2z)\hat{i} - (2x - 0)\hat{j} + (0 - 2y)\hat{k}$$ $$\nabla \times \vec{F} = -2z\hat{i} - 2x\hat{j} - 2y\hat{k}$$

The field is rotational.

Problem 9: Test for Conservative Field

A force field $\vec{F}$ is conservative if its Curl is zero ($\nabla \times \vec{F} = 0$).

1. Calculate the components of the Curl:

$$\text{Curl}_x = \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial z} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2y) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(x^2z + 2xy) = x^2 - x^2 = 0$$ $$\text{Curl}_y = \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(2xyz + y^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2y) = 2xy - 2xy = 0$$ $$\text{Curl}_z = \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2z + 2xy) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2xyz + y^2) = (2xz + 2y) - (2xz + 2y) = 0$$

2. Result: $\nabla \times \vec{F} = 0\hat{i} + 0\hat{j} + 0\hat{k} = \vec{0}$

Conclusion: Since the Curl is zero, the force field is conservative. This means it can be expressed as the negative gradient of a scalar potential function.

Problem 10: Vector Identity Proof ($\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{F}) = 0$)

1. Start with the Curl of $\vec{F}$ (let $\vec{G} = \nabla \times \vec{F}$):

$$\vec{G} = \left(\frac{\partial F_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial z}\right)\hat{i} + \left(\frac{\partial F_x}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial x}\right)\hat{j} + \left(\frac{\partial F_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial y}\right)\hat{k}$$

2. Apply the Divergence operator $\nabla \cdot \vec{G}$:

$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{F}) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial F_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial z}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial F_x}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial x}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(\frac{\partial F_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial y}\right)$$

3. Expand and regroup terms:

$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{F}) = \left(\frac{\partial^2 F_z}{\partial x \partial y} - \frac{\partial^2 F_z}{\partial y \partial x}\right) + \left(\frac{\partial^2 F_x}{\partial y \partial z} - \frac{\partial^2 F_x}{\partial z \partial y}\right) + \left(\frac{\partial^2 F_y}{\partial z \partial x} - \frac{\partial^2 F_y}{\partial x \partial z}\right)$$

4. Since the second partial derivatives are assumed continuous (Clairaut's Theorem), mixed partials are equal ($\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial a \partial b} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial b \partial a}$). Thus, all bracketed pairs cancel out, yielding:

$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{F}) = 0$$
