Meaning of initials

Abbreviations used in this topic:

  • DC = Direct Current — current that flows in one direction only (e.g. from a battery).
  • AC = Alternating Current — current that reverses direction periodically (e.g. mains supply).
  • EMF = Electromotive Force — voltage produced by a source (battery, generator, or induction); unit: volt (V).
  • N (in transformers) = number of turns of wire in a coil ($N_1$ primary, $N_2$ secondary).
  • V = voltage (potential difference); I = current; P = power ($P = VI$).

1. DC Motor – Structure, main idea & working principle

1.1 Main idea

DC = Direct Current. A DC motor converts electrical energy (from a DC source) into mechanical energy (rotation). Core idea: a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force $\mathbf{F} = I \mathbf{L} \times \mathbf{B}$ ($F = BIL\sin\theta$). A coil in a field experiences a torque that rotates it.

1.2 Working principle

Current from the supply flows through brushes → commutator (split ring) → armature coil. The coil sits in the magnetic field of the stator. The two sides of the coil get opposite forces, producing a torque. The commutator (split ring) reverses the current in the coil every half turn so the torque always acts in the same direction. Brushes are fixed contacts (often carbon) that press against the rotating commutator; they carry DC from the supply into the rotating coil.

What is a brush? What does it do?
A brush is a fixed block (often carbon or metal) that presses against the rotating part (commutator or slip ring). Role: it carries current between the fixed wires (supply or load) and the rotating coil, so the coil can turn while staying connected to the circuit. In a DC motor there are two brushes (+ and −); they touch the commutator so DC flows into the coil as it spins.

1.3 Structure diagram

DC motor — main parts (simplified side view)

  • Stator — fixed N–S magnets create the field.
  • Rotor (armature) — coil (green) carries current and rotates.
  • Commutator — split ring (two halves). As the coil turns, the brushes contact opposite halves every half turn, so the current in the coil reverses → torque always in one direction (DC motor).
  • Brushes — fixed blocks that slide on the commutator; they carry DC from the supply to the rotating coil.

1.5 Back EMF

When the coil rotates, it cuts flux → induced EMF opposes the supply (back EMF $\mathcal{E}_b$). So $I = (V - \mathcal{E}_b)/R$. Under load the motor slows, $\mathcal{E}_b$ drops, current increases.

2. AC Generator – Structure, main idea & working principle

2.1 Main idea

AC = Alternating Current. An AC generator (alternator) converts mechanical energy (rotation) into electrical energy as AC. Core idea: changing magnetic flux through a coil induces an EMF (Faraday’s law). Rotating a coil in a fixed field (or a magnet past a coil) makes the flux change periodically → sinusoidal AC output.

2.2 Working principle

The coil (or magnet) is rotated by an external force (turbine, engine). Flux through the coil is $\Phi = NBA\cos(\omega t)$. Faraday’s law gives $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi/dt = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t)$ → sinusoidal AC. Slip rings are full rings (no split). Brushes press against the slip rings and carry the AC from the rotating coil to the fixed load wires. Unlike the DC motor, there is no reversal of which brush touches which part of the ring — so the voltage from the coil is delivered as-is (AC).

Difference: commutator vs slip rings, and the role of brushes
DC motor: Commutator = split ring (two halves). Brushes touch the two halves; as the coil rotates, the connection to + and − swaps every half turn, so the current in the coil reverses → torque always the same direction. Brushes carry DC from the supply into the coil.
AC generator: Slip rings = full rings (no split). Each end of the coil is connected to one ring. Brushes just take the voltage from the rotating coil to the load. The voltage from the coil is already AC; brushes do not reverse anything — they only provide a sliding contact so the rotating coil stays connected to the fixed circuit.

2.3 Structure diagram

AC generator — coil, slip rings (full rings), brushes

  • Stator — fixed N–S magnets create the field.
  • Rotor — coil (purple) is rotated; flux through it changes → EMF is AC.
  • Slip rings — full rings (no split). The coil’s AC is fed out through them; no reversal of connection.
  • Brushes — fixed blocks sliding on the slip rings; they carry the AC from the rotating coil to the fixed output wires.

The coil above rotates; flux $\Phi$ through it varies as $\cos(\omega t)$ → EMF $\mathcal{E} = \mathcal{E}_0\sin(\omega t)$. Output is sinusoidal AC.

Formula: $\mathcal{E} = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t)$, peak $\mathcal{E}_0 = NBA\omega$.

4. Transformers – Main idea, working principle & types

4.1 Main idea

A transformer has two coils (primary and secondary) on a common iron core. AC in the primary produces a changing magnetic flux in the core; by Faraday’s law this induces an EMF in the secondary. So electrical energy is transferred from primary to secondary without direct electrical connection — only by magnetic coupling.

4.2 Working principle

Primary AC current $I_1$ creates a time-varying flux $\Phi$ in the core. The same $\Phi$ links the secondary. So $V_1 \approx -N_1 d\Phi/dt$ and $V_2 = -N_2 d\Phi/dt$, hence $\frac{V_2}{V_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}$. For an ideal transformer (no losses): $V_1 I_1 = V_2 I_2$, so $\frac{I_2}{I_1} = \frac{N_1}{N_2}$. It only works with AC because DC gives constant $\Phi$ and $d\Phi/dt = 0$ → no induced voltage.

4.3 Structure diagrams — two common types

Transformers can be wound in different ways. Two common structures:

(B) Coil inside coil (concentric)

  • Secondary — coil where EMF is induced ($V_2$, $N_2$ turns).
  • Core — iron (or ferrite) links flux between coils.

4.4 How they connect — with transformers

Main flow: AC generator → step-up transformer → grid → step-down transformer → AC loads. DC motors need DC, so they are fed from a rectifier (AC→DC) after the step-down transformer.

4.5 Types

  • Step-up — $N_2 > N_1$ ⇒ $V_2 > V_1$. Used at power stations to raise voltage for transmission.
  • Step-down — $N_2 < N_1$ ⇒ $V_2 < V_1$. Used to lower voltage for consumers.
  • Isolation — $N_2 = N_1$; same voltage, galvanic isolation.
$\frac{V_2}{V_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}$, \quad $V_1 I_1 = V_2 I_2$

5. How AC generator, transformer & DC motor connect

In the grid: AC generator produces AC → step-up transformer raises voltage for transmission → power lines (grid) → step-down transformer lowers voltage → consumers. DC motors need DC, so they are fed from a rectifier (which converts AC to DC) after the step-down transformer. So: AC generator → step-up transformer → grid → step-down transformer → AC loads; and step-down transformer → rectifier → DC motor.

6. Simulations & comparison

Summary of equipment, types, similarities and differences:

EquipmentMain functionTypes / notes
DC motorElectrical → mechanical (rotation)Brushed (commutator); brushless (electronic). Needs DC supply.
AC generatorMechanical → electrical (AC)Slip rings; often rotor = magnet, stator = coil (large machines).
TransformerAC voltage step-up or step-downStep-up, step-down, isolation. AC only.

Similarities: All use magnetic fields and coils. Motor and generator are “inverse”: motor (current → motion), generator (motion → current). Transformer couples two AC circuits magnetically.

Differences: DC motor uses commutator and DC; AC generator uses slip rings and outputs AC. Transformer has no moving parts and only works with AC; it does not convert mechanical ↔ electrical.

7. Transformer worked examples

Use $\frac{V_2}{V_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}$ and $V_1 I_1 = V_2 I_2$.

Example 1. Primary 200 turns, 120 V. Secondary 50 turns. $V_2 = 120 \cdot \frac{50}{200} = 30\ \text{V}$ (step-down).
Example 2. Step-up 240 V → 1200 V, $N_1 = 100$. $N_2 = 100 \cdot \frac{1200}{240} = 500$ turns.
Example 3. $V_1 = 220\ \text{V}$, $I_1 = 2\ \text{A}$, $V_2 = 44\ \text{V}$. $P = 440\ \text{W}$, $I_2 = 440/44 = 10\ \text{A}$.

8. Fifteen transformer problems

Ideal transformers. Use $\frac{V_2}{V_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}$ and $V_1 I_1 = V_2 I_2$.

1. Primary 400 turns, 200 V. Secondary 100 turns. Find $V_2$ and type.
2. Step-down 240 V → 24 V. Primary 600 turns. Find $N_2$.
3. $V_1 = 110\ \text{V}$, $N_1 = 220$, $N_2 = 880$. Find $V_2$ and type.
4. Secondary 12 V, 5 A. Primary 240 V. Find $I_1$ and power.
5. Step-up 120 V → 600 V. Secondary 500 turns. Find $N_1$.
6. $N_1 = 300$, $N_2 = 75$, $V_1 = 120\ \text{V}$. Find $V_2$ and type.
7. Power output 1.2 kW at 48 V. Primary 240 V. Find $I_1$, $I_2$.
8. $V_2 = 36\ \text{V}$, $N_2 = 90$, $N_1 = 360$. Find $V_1$ and type.
9. Primary 2 A, 220 V. Secondary 44 V. Find secondary current and power.
10. Step-down 400 V → 80 V. $N_1 = 1000$. Find $N_2$.
11. $N_1 = 150$, $N_2 = 600$, $V_2 = 400\ \text{V}$. Find $V_1$ and type.
12. Load 0.5 kW at 24 V. Primary 240 V. Find $I_1$.
13. $V_1 = 230\ \text{V}$, $V_2 = 46\ \text{V}$, $N_2 = 100$. Find $N_1$ and type.
14. Secondary 6 V, 10 A. Primary 120 V. Find $I_1$ and type.
15. $N_1 = 500$, $N_2 = 500$, $V_1 = 200\ \text{V}$. Find $V_2$ and type.
