Nuclear Physics — Topic 1

Elements, atomic & mass numbers, stable vs unstable nuclei, terminology, strong force, and finding proton, neutron, electron numbers and masses

1. Element numbers and notation

A nucleus is written as \( {}^A_Z X \), where:

  • \( Z \) = atomic number = number of protons (defines the element).
  • \( N \) = number of neutrons (not shown in the symbol; we get it from \( N = A - Z \)).
  • \( A \) = mass number = total number of nucleons (protons + neutrons), so \( A = Z + N \).
\[ A = Z + N \quad \Rightarrow \quad N = A - Z \]

In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals \( Z \). If the atom is ionised, electron count = \( Z - \text{(charge in units of } e\text{)} \).

2. Stable and unstable (radioactive) elements

Stable nuclei do not decay; they exist indefinitely. Unstable (radioactive) nuclei spontaneously decay (alpha, beta, or gamma) to become more stable.

Stability depends on the ratio of neutrons to protons and on the total size of the nucleus. Light stable nuclei often have \( N \approx Z \). Heavier stable nuclei need more neutrons than protons (e.g. lead, uranium). Nuclei that are too proton-rich, neutron-rich, or too heavy tend to be unstable.

Examples: \( {}^4_2 \text{He} \), \( {}^{12}_6 \text{C} \), \( {}^{16}_8 \text{O} \) are stable. \( {}^{238}_{92} \text{U} \) is unstable (alpha decay). \( {}^14_6 \text{C} \) is unstable (beta minus decay).

3. Terminology

  • Nucleon: a proton or a neutron.
  • Isotopes: same \( Z \), different \( A \) (same element, different number of neutrons).
  • Mass number \( A \): integer (protons + neutrons).
  • Atomic mass (in u or kg): measured mass of the atom/nucleus; approximately \( A \times 1\,\text{u} \) where \( 1\,\text{u} \approx 1.66 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg} \).
  • Binding energy: energy needed to break the nucleus into separate nucleons; related to mass defect via \( E = \Delta m\, c^2 \).

4. Strong (nuclear) force

The strong force holds protons and neutrons together in the nucleus. It is:

  • Short range — acts only over roughly 1–3 fm (femtometres); outside that range it is negligible.
  • Stronger than the electrostatic repulsion between protons at short range, so the nucleus does not fly apart.
  • Acts between nucleons (proton–proton, neutron–neutron, proton–neutron).

For large nuclei, the short range means nucleons at the surface feel fewer neighbours, so binding per nucleon drops and very heavy nuclei can become unstable (e.g. fission or alpha decay).

5. How to find proton, neutron, and electron numbers and masses

From the symbol \( {}^A_Z X \):

\[ \text{Protons: } Z, \qquad \text{Neutrons: } N = A - Z, \qquad \text{Electrons (neutral atom): } Z \]

For an ion with charge \( +q \) in units of \( e \) (e.g. \( +2 \)), electron count = \( Z - q \). For \( -q \), electron count = \( Z + q \).

Masses (approximate):

  • Proton mass \( m_p \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg} \approx 1\,\text{u} \).
  • Neutron mass \( m_n \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg} \approx 1\,\text{u} \).
  • Electron mass \( m_e \approx 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\,\text{kg} \approx 0.00055\,\text{u} \) (often negligible for nuclear mass).
  • Nuclear mass \( \approx A \times 1\,\text{u} \) (slightly less due to binding energy).
Example: \( {}^{35}_{17} \text{Cl} \). Protons = 17, neutrons = 35 − 17 = 18, electrons (neutral) = 17. Approximate mass of nucleus \( \approx 35\,\text{u} \).
