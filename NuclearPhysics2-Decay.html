Nuclear Physics — Topic 2

Alpha, beta and gamma decay: how they work, why they happen, and energy & radiation

1. Alpha decay (\( \alpha \))

The nucleus emits an alpha particle, which is a \( {}^4_2 \text{He} \) nucleus (2 protons, 2 neutrons).

\[ {}^A_Z X \;\to\; {}^{A-4}_{Z-2} Y + {}^4_2 \text{He} \quad (\alpha) \]

Why it happens: Heavy nuclei have many protons repelling each other. Emitting a tightly bound alpha reduces Coulomb repulsion and moves the nucleus toward the “valley of stability.” The process is allowed when the total mass of the products is less than the mass of the parent (so energy is released).

Behaviour: Alpha particles are heavy and doubly charged; they ionise strongly but penetrate only a few cm in air or a sheet of paper. They are stopped easily and are dangerous only if the source is inside the body.

Energy: The decay energy \( Q \) appears as kinetic energy of the alpha and the recoiling daughter nucleus (and sometimes a gamma). \( Q = (m_\text{parent} - m_\text{daughter} - m_\alpha)c^2 \). Typically a few MeV.

Example: \( {}^{238}_{92} \text{U} \to {}^{234}_{90} \text{Th} + {}^4_2 \text{He} \). Mass number: 238 = 234 + 4. Charge: 92 = 90 + 2.

2. Beta decay (\( \beta^- \), \( \beta^+ \), electron capture)

Beta minus (\( \beta^- \)): A neutron in the nucleus turns into a proton, emitting an electron and an antineutrino:

\[ {}^A_Z X \;\to\; {}^A_{Z+1} Y + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e \]

The daughter has the same \( A \) but \( Z \) increases by 1. This happens in neutron-rich nuclei to move toward the stability line.

Beta plus (\( \beta^+ \)): A proton turns into a neutron, emitting a positron and a neutrino: \( {}^A_Z X \to {}^A_{Z-1} Y + e^+ + \nu_e \). Occurs in proton-rich nuclei.

Why it happens: The weak interaction changes a quark (e.g. down → up in \( \beta^- \)), converting a neutron to a proton or vice versa. The nucleus moves toward the optimal \( N/Z \) ratio.

Behaviour: Beta particles are light and charged; they penetrate more than alpha (metres in air, mm in tissue) and cause ionisation. They have a continuous energy spectrum (from 0 up to \( Q \)) because the energy is shared with the neutrino.

Energy: \( Q = (m_\text{parent} - m_\text{daughter})c^2 \) (minus electron mass for \( \beta^- \)). The maximum kinetic energy of the beta equals \( Q \).

3. Gamma decay (\( \gamma \))

The nucleus is in an excited state (higher energy level) and drops to a lower state by emitting a gamma-ray photon (high-energy electromagnetic wave).

\[ {}^A_Z X^* \;\to\; {}^A_Z X + \gamma \]

\( Z \) and \( A \) do not change — it is the same nucleus, just losing internal energy. No particles are ejected; only photons.

Why it happens: After alpha or beta decay, the daughter nucleus is often left in an excited state. It relaxes by emitting one or more gamma photons. So gamma often accompanies alpha or beta.

Behaviour: Gamma rays are uncharged and very penetrating (many cm of lead or concrete to attenuate). They ionise indirectly (e.g. by knocking out electrons) and are dangerous externally because they pass through skin and tissue.

Energy: \( E_\gamma = hf = \Delta E \), where \( \Delta E \) is the energy difference between the two nuclear levels. Typically keV to MeV.

4. Summary: radiation and energy

  • Alpha: nucleus loses 2p + 2n; high ionisation, low penetration; energy \( Q \) shared between alpha and daughter.
  • Beta: nucleus changes \( Z \) by ±1, \( A \) unchanged; continuous spectrum; energy \( Q \) shared between beta and neutrino.
  • Gamma: same nucleus, lower energy state; photon carries \( \Delta E \); high penetration, no mass or charge change.

All three are forms of ionising radiation: they can knock electrons out of atoms and damage tissue. Shielding and distance are used to reduce exposure.
