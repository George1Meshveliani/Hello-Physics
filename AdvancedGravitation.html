A Calculus-Based Exploration of Gravitation

1. Newton’s Law of Gravitation

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation provides the foundational framework for understanding celestial mechanics. It posits that the gravitational force between two point masses is attractive, directly proportional to the product of their masses, and inversely proportional to the square of their separation distance.

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = - G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \hat{r}_{12} $$

The associated gravitational field $\vec{g}$ created by a mass $M$ is a vector field representing the force per unit mass at any point in space. The field is a property of space itself, modified by the presence of mass.

$$ \vec{g}(\vec{r}) = -G \frac{M}{r^2} \hat{r} $$

For a collection of masses, the total gravitational field at a point is the vector sum of the fields from each individual mass, an application of the Principle of Superposition.

$$ \vec{g}_{total} = \sum_i \vec{g}_i = -G \sum_i \frac{M_i}{r_i^2} \hat{r}_i $$

2. Weight

Weight is the gravitational force exerted on an object by a large astronomical body. For an object at a height $h$ above the Earth's surface, its weight is given by:

$$ W(h) = F_g = G \frac{M_E m}{(R_E + h)^2} = mg(h) $$

Proof: Variation of $g$ with Altitude

For altitudes $h \ll R_E$, a first-order approximation can be found using the binomial expansion $(1+x)^n \approx 1+nx$ for small $x$.

$$ g(h) = G \frac{M_E}{R_E^2(1 + h/R_E)^2} = g_0 (1 + h/R_E)^{-2} $$

Where $g_0 = GM_E/R_E^2$ is the acceleration at the surface. Using the binomial approximation with $x=h/R_E$ and $n=-2$:

$$ g(h) \approx g_0 \left(1 - 2\frac{h}{R_E}\right) $$

This linear approximation is highly accurate for altitudes up to about 100 km.

Proof: Variation of $g$ with Depth

Assuming the Earth has a uniform density $\rho$, the gravitational force on a mass $m$ at a distance $r$ from the center ($r < R_E$) is due only to the mass enclosed within the sphere of radius $r$, according to the Shell Theorem. The mass enclosed is $M_{enc} = \rho \cdot V_r = \frac{M_E}{(4/3)\pi R_E^3} \cdot \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 = M_E \frac{r^3}{R_E^3}$.

The gravitational force is $F_g(r) = G \frac{M_{enc}m}{r^2} = G \frac{(M_E r^3/R_E^3)m}{r^2} = \left(\frac{GM_E m}{R_E^3}\right) r$.

Since $F_g = mg(r)$, the gravitational acceleration inside the Earth is:

$$ g(r) = \left(\frac{GM_E}{R_E^3}\right) r = g_0 \frac{r}{R_E} $$

This shows that $g$ decreases linearly as one approaches the center of the Earth, becoming zero at the very center.

3. Gravitational Potential Energy

Since the gravitational force is conservative, we can define a scalar potential energy function $U$ such that the work done by gravity is the negative change in potential energy, $W_g = -\Delta U$.

$$ \Delta U = U_f - U_i = - \int_{r_i}^{r_f} \vec{F}_g \cdot d\vec{r} = -G M m \left(\frac{1}{r_f} - \frac{1}{r_i}\right) $$

By defining the zero point of potential energy at infinite separation ($U(\infty) = 0$), the potential energy of a two-mass system is always negative:

$$ U(r) = - G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r} $$

4. The Motion of Satellites

For a satellite in a circular orbit, the gravitational force provides the necessary centripetal force. This balance dictates the satellite's speed and energy.

Proof: Total Energy of a Circular Orbit

From Newton's second law for circular motion: $G \frac{Mm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$.

The kinetic energy is $K = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} m \left(\frac{GM}{r}\right) = \frac{GMm}{2r}$.

The potential energy is $U = - \frac{GMm}{r}$.

The total energy is the sum, a result known as the Virial Theorem for this system:

$$ E = K + U = \frac{GMm}{2r} - \frac{GMm}{r} = - \frac{GMm}{2r} \quad (\text{Note: } E = -K = U/2) $$

The negative energy signifies a bound system. An object with $E \ge 0$ is unbound and will follow a parabolic or hyperbolic trajectory, escaping the gravitational pull.

Proof: Escape Velocity

Escape velocity is the minimum speed an object must have at a given distance $R$ from a mass $M$ to escape its gravitational influence, i.e., to reach $r=\infty$ with zero final speed. We use conservation of energy. The minimum energy for escape is $E_{total}=0$.

Initial energy at the surface (radius R) must equal the final energy at infinity:

$$ E_i = E_f \implies \frac{1}{2}mv_{esc}^2 - \frac{GMm}{R} = 0 + 0 $$

Solving for $v_{esc}$ gives:

$$ v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}} $$

Note that this is $\sqrt{2}$ times the speed required for a circular orbit at that radius.

5. Kepler’s Laws and the Motion of Planets

[Image of a planetary elliptical orbit with foci]

Proof of Kepler's First Law (Law of Orbits)

The derivation of elliptical orbits from Newton's laws is a classic problem in mechanics. It involves solving the vector equation of motion $\vec{F}=m\vec{a}$ for a central force. Using polar coordinates, the equation of motion for the reduced mass $\mu$ is:

$$ \mu(\ddot{r} - r\dot{\theta}^2) = -G\frac{M\mu}{r^2} \quad \text{and} \quad \mu(r\ddot{\theta} + 2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}) = 0 $$

The second equation implies conservation of angular momentum, $L = \mu r^2 \dot{\theta} = \text{const}$. This can be used to eliminate $\dot{\theta}$ from the radial equation. The substitution $u=1/r$ transforms the radial equation into a simpler form (Binet's equation):

$$ \frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2} + u = \frac{GM\mu^2}{L^2} $$

This is the equation for a simple harmonic oscillator with a constant term. Its solution is $u(\theta) = A\cos(\theta - \theta_0) + \frac{GM\mu^2}{L^2}$. Converting back to $r=1/u$ gives the equation for a conic section:

$$ r(\theta) = \frac{L^2/(GM\mu^2)}{1 + \epsilon \cos(\theta-\theta_0)} $$

where $\epsilon = AL^2/(GM\mu^2)$ is the eccentricity. For a bound orbit, $0 \le \epsilon < 1$, which defines an ellipse. This proves planets move in elliptical orbits.

Proof of Kepler's Second Law (Law of Areas)

This law is a direct consequence of the conservation of angular momentum. Since gravity is a central force, the torque $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}_g$ is zero, which implies the angular momentum $\vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p}$ is constant.

The infinitesimal area $dA$ swept by the position vector $\vec{r}$ in time $dt$ is $dA = \frac{1}{2} |\vec{r} \times d\vec{r}| = \frac{1}{2} |\vec{r} \times \vec{v}dt|$. The rate of change of area is:

$$ \frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{1}{2} |\vec{r} \times \vec{v}| = \frac{|\vec{L}|}{2m} = \text{constant} $$

Proof of Kepler's Third Law (Law of Periods)

The total area of an ellipse is $A = \pi ab$, where $a$ is the semi-major axis and $b$ is the semi-minor axis. The orbital period is $T = A / (dA/dt)$.

Using $dA/dt = L/2m$, we get $T = \frac{2m(\pi ab)}{L}$. For an ellipse, $b=a\sqrt{1-\epsilon^2}$ and the angular momentum can be shown to be $L = \sqrt{GMm^2 a(1-\epsilon^2)}$.

Substituting these into the period equation:

$$ T = \frac{2m\pi a^2 \sqrt{1-\epsilon^2}}{\sqrt{GMm^2 a(1-\epsilon^2)}} = \frac{2\pi a^{3/2}}{\sqrt{GM}} $$

Squaring both sides gives the celebrated result:

$$ T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} a^3 \quad \implies \quad T^2 \propto a^3 $$

6. Spherical Mass Distributions

Proof of Shell Theorem via Direct Integration

We prove that the gravitational force outside a uniform spherical shell of mass $M$ is the same as if all its mass were at its center by calculating the potential.

Consider a ring on the shell at an angle $\theta$. The mass of the ring is $dM = \frac{M}{2}\sin\theta d\theta$. Every point on this ring is at a distance $s$ from the external point mass $m$. From the Law of Cosines, $s^2 = r^2 + R^2 - 2rR\cos\theta$.

The potential from the ring is $dU = -G \frac{m \, dM}{s}$. By changing variables from $\theta$ to $s$ (where $2s\,ds = 2rR\sin\theta d\theta$) and integrating over the entire shell (from $s=r-R$ to $s=r+R$ for a point outside), we find:

$$ U = \int dU = \int_{r-R}^{r+R} -G\frac{m(M/2rR)s\,ds}{s} = -\frac{GMm}{2rR} \int_{r-R}^{r+R} ds $$
$$ U_{out} = - \frac{GMm}{r} $$

For a point inside the shell, the limits of integration are $R-r$ to $R+r$, which yields a constant potential: $U_{in} = -GMm/R$. Since force is the negative gradient of potential, $F = -dU/dr$, the force inside the shell is zero.

Proof of Shell Theorem via Gauss's Law for Gravity

A more elegant proof uses Gauss's Law for Gravity, which is analogous to Gauss's Law in electrostatics. It states that the flux of the gravitational field through any closed surface is proportional to the enclosed mass.

$$ \oint \vec{g} \cdot d\vec{A} = -4\pi G M_{enc} $$

Outside the shell ($r > R$): We choose a spherical Gaussian surface of radius $r$. By symmetry, $\vec{g}$ must be radial and have constant magnitude on the surface. So, $\oint \vec{g} \cdot d\vec{A} = g(4\pi r^2)$. The enclosed mass is the entire shell, $M_{enc}=M$.

$$ g(4\pi r^2) = -4\pi GM \implies g = -G\frac{M}{r^2} $$

Inside the shell ($r < R$): We choose a spherical Gaussian surface of radius $r < R$. The enclosed mass is zero, $M_{enc}=0$.

$$ g(4\pi r^2) = -4\pi G (0) \implies g=0 $$

This method elegantly proves both parts of the Shell Theorem.

7. Apparent Weight and the Earth’s Rotation

An object on the surface of the rotating Earth is in a non-inertial reference frame. This causes its apparent weight (the reading on a scale) to differ from its true gravitational weight.

The net force on an object at latitude $\lambda$ provides the centripetal force required for its circular path of radius $r_c = R_E \cos\lambda$ around the Earth's axis.

$$ \vec{F}_{net} = \vec{F}_g + \vec{F}_N = m\vec{a}_c $$

The apparent weight is the normal force, $\vec{W}_{app} = \vec{F}_N = m\vec{g}_{true} - m\vec{a}_c$. The centripetal acceleration, $a_c = \omega^2 (R_E \cos\lambda)$, is directed perpendicular to the axis of rotation. This subtractive term is largest at the equator ($\lambda=0$) and zero at the poles ($\lambda=90^\circ$). The vector subtraction also means the apparent weight is not pointed directly towards the Earth's center (except at the equator and poles).

Advanced Problems in Gravitation

These problems are designed to test your understanding of the material at a deep, analytical level. They require a solid foundation in calculus and classical mechanics.

Intermediate Problems

1. Hohmann Transfer Orbit Analysis

A satellite in a 7,000 km radius circular Earth orbit must be transferred to a 42,200 km radius circular geosynchronous orbit. The transfer is to be achieved via an elliptical Hohmann orbit. This maneuver requires two instantaneous engine burns. The first burn places the satellite onto the transfer ellipse at its periapsis, and the second burn circularizes the orbit at its apoapsis. Calculate the total propulsive velocity change, $\Delta v_{total} = \Delta v_1 + \Delta v_2$, required for this orbital transfer.

2. Oscillatory Motion in a Gravity Tunnel

Consider an idealized Earth of uniform density $\rho$, mass $M_E$, and radius $R_E$. A frictionless tunnel is bored along a chord of the Earth, not necessarily through the center. An object of mass $m$ is released from the surface into this tunnel. Prove that the object experiences a linear restoring force and subsequently undergoes simple harmonic motion. Derive an expression for the period of this oscillation and show it is independent of the chord's location.

3. The Sun-Earth L1 Lagrange Point

In a system with two large bodies (e.g., Sun and Earth) orbiting their common center of mass, there exist five points of stability known as Lagrange points. The L1 point lies on the line connecting the two bodies. A satellite placed at L1 will orbit the Sun with the same angular velocity as the Earth. Consider the Sun (mass $M_S$) and Earth (mass $M_E$) separated by distance $R$. In the rotating reference frame, find the distance $r$ from the Earth to the L1 point by balancing gravitational and centrifugal forces.

4. Energy Requirement for Orbital Insertion

A 1,200 kg satellite is initially in a low Earth circular orbit at an altitude of 300 km. It needs to be placed into a final geostationary circular orbit at an altitude of 35,786 km. Calculate the minimum work that must be supplied by the satellite's propulsion system to achieve this change in orbit. Ignore the mass of the expended fuel.

5. Dynamics of a Binary Star System

Two stars of masses $M_A$ and $M_B$ are gravitationally bound in a binary system. They follow stable circular orbits around their common center of mass with a constant separation distance $d$. Derive a general expression for the orbital period $T$ of the system in terms of $M_A$, $M_B$, $d$, and the gravitational constant $G$.

Hard Problems

6. Gravitational Self-Energy of a Sphere

Derive the total gravitational potential energy (self-energy) of a uniform sphere of mass $M$ and radius $R$. This quantity represents the work done to assemble the sphere by bringing infinitesimal shells of mass from an infinite distance. The final expression should be in terms of $G$, $M$, and $R$.

7. The Gravitational Slingshot

A spacecraft of mass $m$ approaches a planet of mass $M$ ($M \gg m$). In the Sun's inertial frame, the planet moves with velocity $\vec{V}$ and the spacecraft has initial velocity $\vec{v}_i$. The maneuver is designed such that the spacecraft performs a hyperbolic fly-by. In the planet's rest frame, the encounter is elastic, meaning the spacecraft's speed is unchanged. If the initial approach is head-on (velocities are anti-parallel), determine the spacecraft's final velocity $\vec{v}_f$ in the Sun's frame and show that its kinetic energy increases by $2m|\vec{V}|(|\vec{v}_i| + |\vec{V}|)$.

8. Gravitational Field of a Finite Rod

A uniform, thin rod of mass $M$ and length $L$ is placed along the x-axis, centered at the origin. By integrating the contributions from infinitesimal mass elements, derive the exact expression for the gravitational field vector $\vec{g}$ at a point $P$ on the positive y-axis, a distance $y$ from the origin.

9. Orbital Stability with a Perturbation Potential

The gravitational potential energy of a test mass $m$ in the field of a non-uniform planet is given by $U(r) = -\frac{GMm}{r}\left(1 + \frac{\alpha}{r^2}\right)$, where $\alpha$ is a constant. First, derive the force law $F(r)$. Using the concept of an effective potential energy $U_{eff}(r)$ for a given angular momentum $L$, find the condition on $\alpha$ that must be met for stable circular orbits to exist.

10. Tidal Stress on a Radially Infalling Rod

A rigid, uniform rod of mass $m$, length $L$, and cross-sectional area $A$ is in a radial free-fall towards a planet of mass $M_P$. The center of the rod is at a distance $r$ from the planet's center ($r \gg L$). The rod remains aligned radially. Derive an expression for the tensile stress (internal tension force per unit area) at the midpoint of the rod caused by the planet's gravitational gradient (tidal forces).

Advanced (Irodov-like) Problems

11. Escape Velocity from a Rotating Body

An object is located at the equator of a spherical asteroid of radius $R$ and uniform density $\rho$, which rotates with a constant angular velocity $\omega$. Determine the minimum launch speed $v_{rel}$ relative to the asteroid's surface required for the object to escape to infinity. You must also determine the optimal launch direction (e.g., prograde, retrograde, normal) that minimizes this speed.

12. Bertrand's Theorem and Orbital Stability

For a central force of the form $F(r) = -k/r^n$, only two values of the exponent $n$ result in stable, closed orbits for all bound initial conditions. One is $n=2$ (gravity/electrostatics). Using an analysis of the effective potential, find the other integer value of $n$. This is a specific case of Bertrand's Theorem.

13. Field Inside a Spherical Cavity

Consider a solid sphere of uniform density $\rho$ and radius $R$. A spherical cavity of radius $a$ is carved out from it. The center of the cavity is displaced from the center of the large sphere by a vector $\vec{b}$. Using the principle of superposition, prove that the gravitational field inside the cavity is uniform (both in magnitude and direction) and find its vector value.

14. Derivation of the Roche Limit

A small, fluid satellite of mean density $\rho_m$ and radius $r_m$ orbits a large planet of mean density $\rho_p$ and radius $R_p$. The satellite is held together by its own gravity. The Roche limit is the orbital distance $d$ at which the planet's tidal forces are strong enough to disintegrate the satellite. By modeling the satellite as two test masses separated by $2r_m$ and comparing the planet's differential gravitational force to the satellite's self-gravitational force, derive the expression for the Roche limit: $d \approx R_p \left(2 \frac{\rho_p}{\rho_m}\right)^{1/3}$.

15. Apsidal Precession from a Perturbation

A planet's potential energy includes a small perturbation term from General Relativity, giving $U(r) = -\frac{k}{r} + \frac{h}{r^2}$, where $k=GMm$ and $h$ is a small constant. This causes the apsides (perihelion and aphelion) of the orbit to precess. For a nearly circular orbit, calculate the angle $\Delta\phi$ by which the perihelion advances per revolution. (Hint: The period of radial oscillations will be slightly different from the orbital period).
